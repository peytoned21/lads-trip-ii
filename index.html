A Declaration of Intent

The Lads Trip II

Outer Banks, North Carolina

When April 15–18, 2027
Where Kill Devil Hills, NC
Est. Cost ~$900–1,100
↓ scroll

The Outer Banks doesn't look like anything else on the East Coast. It looks like the edge of something — a 200-mile strip of barrier island where the Atlantic decides to stop being the ocean and start being the beach. Narrow enough in places that you can see water on both sides from the same window. Wide enough in others that the dunes swallow the horizon. The Wright Brothers flew here in December 1903. The wild horses still run loose up the road in Corolla. History does what it always does on the OBX: it just sits there being true, unbothered, waiting to be noticed.

The golf is links-style, which means wind in your face and actual decisions to make on every tee. Sea Scape is ten minutes from the house and lets everyone find their game. The Pointe is twenty minutes north and is where the game gets serious. Four courses within 45 minutes of the front door. The seafood comes straight from the Pamlico Sound. The houses sit directly on the water — not a dune walk away, on it, above it, with the Atlantic in every window from the top floor down.

The Outer Banks is the edge of the country. The water on the left is the sound. The water on the right is the Atlantic. The house is somewhere in between. That is the trip.

Kill Devil Hills is the base — a beach town with enough bars and restaurants that Saturday night doesn't require a bus or a plan. Norfolk is an hour and fifteen minutes from the house if you fly. From Atlanta it is nine hours if you drive, which some will. The wild horses are in Corolla, forty-five minutes north, if anyone needs a Sunday morning reason to go slow.

The OBX has been doing exactly this for fifty years. Big houses on the water. Good golf nearby. Fresh seafood. A town that stays open late. It knows what it is. The question is whether we show up.

"The years were not going to take this from them without a fight."

— The Lads Trip I, Gulf Shores, March 2026

Myrtle Beach does not need your approval. It has been hosting groups exactly like ours for sixty years — men with handicaps they haven't updated, coolers they filled at a gas station, and a group chat that went sideways before they hit the state line. It knows what it is. The question is whether you do.

The pitch is simple and honest: Myrtle Beach is easier to get to, cheaper to operate, and has more golf within fifteen minutes of the house than most coastal destinations have within an hour. It is the trip you can actually afford to do right. The trip where the cost savings go into rounds instead of the rental. The trip where nobody has to justify the flight.

Murrells Inlet is the part of Myrtle Beach nobody talks about on the highway billboards. Thirty minutes south of the strip, past the mini-golf and the Ripley's, where the marsh opens up and the water turns serious and the seafood comes in from the boats that morning. The MarshWalk runs right along the inlet — open-air, waterfront, cold beer, the kind of sunset that makes a man quiet for a minute before he orders another round.

Eighty courses within forty minutes of each other. The Golf Capital of the World is not a marketing claim. It is a logistics advantage.

Caledonia is the one you tell people about when you get home. TPC Myrtle Beach is a Tom Fazio design that hosted the Senior PGA Tour Championship and sits five miles from the house. True Blue is the round that breaks someone. These are not consolation courses. These are the rounds that justify the trip, and there are more of them within forty minutes of the house than most golf destinations can offer in a weekend.

The chaos isn't in the destination. It travels with us. Myrtle Beach will have its own version of all of it. It always does.

The Civilized Option

Every group has one trip where they look back and say: that was the one where we did it right. Not louder, not cheaper, not more golf — right. Isle of Palms is that trip. It is what happens when you take everything this group loves and put it inside a place that was designed, over three centuries, to be worth the journey.

The island itself is a seven-mile barrier island thirty minutes northeast of downtown Charleston. Wide Atlantic beach, palmetto trees, marsh on the western side catching the last light of the afternoon. The houses on Isle of Palms are the kind with proper wraparound porches and outdoor showers and the ocean visible from the kitchen window. Wild Dunes Resort sits on the eastern tip — two Tom Fazio courses, the Links and the Harbor, built into the dunes with the Atlantic framing the finishing hole of the Links in a way Fazio himself has called one of his favorites.

And then there is Charleston. Forty-five minutes from Kiawah Island and the Ocean Course — Pete Dye's masterpiece, host of the 1991 Ryder Cup, the 2012 and 2021 PGA Championships, ranked among the hardest courses in America. The city itself is a night out unlike anything else on the South Carolina coast. King Street. The bars in the French Quarter. Husk. FIG. The kind of dinner that requires a reservation and justifies the effort.

The Ocean Course at Kiawah is the single most significant public golf course in America. The 1991 Ryder Cup happened here. Both the 2012 and 2021 PGA Championships happened here. This is the round you call your father about.

The pitch is not that Isle of Palms is the cheapest or the most convenient. It is neither. The pitch is that fifteen men who have known each other for fifteen years deserve, once in a while, to do it properly. The island. The city. The courses. The dinner. The version of the trip that, twenty years from now, still gets brought up when someone's kid asks what their dad was like when he was young.

That is what Charleston is for.

"Tom Fazio's first golf course design. The finishing hole looks at the Atlantic Ocean. He still says it's one of his favorites."

The Golf Island

When April 15–18, 2027
PGA Tour RBC Heritage Week
Est. Cost ~$950–1,100

Hilton Head Island has been doing exactly one thing since 1969: hosting men who take golf seriously. Sea Pines founder Charles Fraser hired Pete Dye and Jack Nicklaus to build a course, then announced it would host a PGA Tour event the same year it opened. Arnold Palmer won the first one. The tradition has not stopped. Over 100,000 people show up every April. The island has spent sixty years getting ready for a group like ours.

There is a complication worth naming directly. Our dates — April 15 through 18 — land inside RBC Heritage week. The tournament runs April 14 through 20, 2027. Harbour Town is closed to public play during the event. Sea Pines access gets restricted. Rental prices spike. That is the honest picture.

The honest picture also includes this: we would be on Hilton Head Island during one of the best sporting weeks in the American South. 100,000 people in plaid. The best players in the world walking Harbour Town's tight fairways and the par-3 17th over Calibogue Sound. We could buy tickets, watch a round, and still play three other marquee courses — Palmetto Dunes has three on property, and Crescent Pointe in Bluffton is unaffected by tournament restrictions entirely.

Harbour Town is a Pete Dye and Jack Nicklaus design ranked second among all PGA Tour courses by Golf Digest — behind only Augusta National. The week we're there, the best players in the world will be walking the same fairways.

The Salty Dog Café has been on South Beach Marina since 1987. It is open-air, on the water, serves the kind of seafood that comes in that morning, and is the place everybody ends up after dark. It does not need to be anything else.

Here is the honest argument: we would be on one of the great golf islands in America during one of the great golf weeks in American sports. The complication is real. The tradeoff is worth it. Harbour Town closed to public play is still Harbour Town we can walk and watch. The other three courses are unaffected. The island is buzzing in a way it only buzzes one week a year. We would be there for that week, on purpose, knowing exactly what it is. That is not a consolation. That is the pitch.

There is a version of this pitch that sells St. Simons Island on its merits — the Spanish moss, the live oaks, the barrier island beauty, the King and Prince, Crab Daddy's, the marshes at low tide. That pitch is accurate and it is not the one this group needs. This group needs the other pitch. The one that starts with: we have been here before, and it did not go the way anyone planned, and that is exactly why we should go back.

Georgia-Florida weekend. You know this story or you were in it. Peyton got arrested. The specific details of this incident are not included here because they do not need to be — the men who were there remember, and the men who weren't will ask, and that question is the beginning of a very good first night at whatever house we rent. Mack almost drowned. Yang dressed as a Rubik's cube. These are the three sentences that constitute the complete institutional record of the group's prior St. Simons experience, and they are sufficient.

St. Simons Island is 4.5 hours from Atlanta. Nobody flies. Everybody drives. There is no easier trip in this field, and no trip with more unfinished business.

The island itself is Georgia's largest barrier island. Spanish moss draping every oak. The lighthouse has been there since 1872. Fort Frederica is a national monument. Crab Daddy's is on Ocean Boulevard and the shrimp comes from the boats. The King and Prince Golf Course winds through ancient coastal forests and salt marshes — eighteen holes that were carved, hole by hole, out of small marsh islands. The Sea Island Seaside Course is Tom Fazio's redesign of a 1929 classic and is among the top 100 courses in America. It is for resort guests and members, which means we need to either stay at Sea Island or find someone who is a member. This is a problem worth solving.

There is no Flora-Bama equivalent on St. Simons. There is the Village, which is a collection of bars and restaurants around the pier that stays open late and has the right energy. There is Crab Daddy's, which is the answer to every seafood question. There is the beach at East Beach, which is uncrowded and correct. And there is the particular energy of men returning to a place where things went wrong and deciding, collectively, that this time will be different. It probably won't be different. That is also fine.

There is a particular kind of courage required to say: we are going back. Not because we didn't find somewhere better. Not because the imagination failed. Because what happened there was so specifically right that the case for anywhere else is, in the end, a case against something that worked.

Gulf Shores worked. The house worked. Mark and the buckets worked. The golf worked. Flora-Bama was exactly what Flora-Bama always is — sixty years of Saturday nights compressed into one bar, playing early 2000s music in the red light while men who should probably be home hugged each other like they meant it. Connor won the raffle even though he'd gone home. Twin girls are on the way. The Cybertruck was parked diagonally in the driveway of 1477 West Beach Boulevard and it was magnificent and nobody ordered it.

The argument for going back is not nostalgia. The argument is that the first time, you were figuring out how to do this. The second time, you already know. You know which tee time to book and which market to find the crawfish at and which seat at Flora-Bama catches the best light and how to arrange fifteen men in a house so that the morning is quiet and the afternoon is loud and the night is exactly the right length. The first trip built the blueprint. The second trip is what you build with it.

Every group that does this right has one place they return to. The Ryder Cup went back to Kiawah. The Masters goes back to Augusta. We should go back to the Gulf.

Aqua Vista is still there. The tiki bar still faces the Gulf of Mexico. Kiva Dunes is still the course where Kunal and Miguel independently ordered a six-pack each and discovered the coincidence somewhere around the third hole. The pilot is still reachable. We know how to find Mark.

Going somewhere new requires the energy of discovery. Going back requires only the willingness to admit that the first time mattered enough to do again. That is a harder thing. That is character.
